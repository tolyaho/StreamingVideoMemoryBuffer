## 9. Conclusion

Overall, I'm reasonably happy with what came out. The system does everything what was asked for: bounded streaming memory, coarse-to-fine retrieval, and a clean LLM-facing interface. Two end-to-end runs go through the full pipeline — $3/5$ on the $17$ min cooking clip, $8/22$ on the $84$ min LVBench concert.

The architecture turns out to be well-thought and working: three tiers, each with a clear job — recent windows keep the "right now" detail, episodes group coherent action spans, events hold the long-term story — and three captioners matched to what each tier actually needs: Florence-2 on every window because speed matters, Moondream2 on episode flushes because per-frame grounding kills hallucinations, Qwen2.5-VL on event consolidation because that's where text carries most of the weight. Retrieval is coarse-to-fine on top of this, X-CLIP puts video and text in the same space so no projector is needed, and the write path doesn't know anything about future queries — so dropping in a different retriever later doesn't touch storage. 

Along the way I also learned how much small scoring choices matter (multiplicative time decay beating an additive $\gamma$ term, self-centrality pooling beating a plain mean), but those are details — the shape of the architecture is the same.

From my perspective, the biggest remaining gap is the captioner, not the memory. On LVBench the misses are questions where the per-frame caption never wrote the relevant detail down, and retrieval obviously can't recover information that was never stored. The fix isn't in the memory layer; it's in the evidence channel. The `visual_context` slot is already in `format_for_llm`'s output dict, just unused in this demo — routing that into a real VLM reasoner (or training a small MLP projector so the X-CLIP embeddings land in the LLM's token space) would close most of the fine-grained visual gap without touching storage. A learned re-ranker on top of the current $\alpha/\beta/\tau$ is the other obvious next step. If I had more time, that's where I'd spend it.

Thanks for the assignment — genuinely had fun with it.
